## The Philosophy Behind Recognition

Open source thrives on community contributions, yet contributors often struggle to quantify their impact when applying for jobs or building their professional reputation. The OJP Recognition Program addresses this gap by providing **tangible, shareable credentials** that validate your open-source work.

Every badge represents real impactâ€”code that ships, tests that prevent bugs, documentation that helps developers, or advocacy that grows the community. When you earn an OJP badge, you've demonstrated skills and commitment that employers value: collaboration, technical excellence, communication, and the ability to contribute to production systems used by others.

The program is designed with several key principles:

**Community-driven**: Badge nominations come from fellow contributors who've seen your work and impact firsthand. This peer recognition carries weight because it reflects how your contributions are valued by those who work alongside you.

**Multi-track progression**: Not everyone contributes the same way. Some developers excel at code, others at testing, documentation, or community building. OJP recognizes excellence in all forms, with separate progression paths for each contribution type.

**Lightweight and fair**: The nomination and award process avoids bureaucracy. Recognition focuses on impact, not paperwork. The project founder and existing Champions review nominations with discretion, balancing formal criteria with community feedback.

**Publicly verifiable**: All badges link to this document in the OJP repository, providing a permanent, verifiable record of your achievement. Employers or collaborators can click through to see exactly what each badge represents.

## Understanding the Badge System

The OJP Recognition Program organizes badges into four contribution tracks, each with three progressive levels: Entry, Intermediate, and Advanced. All tracks converge at the highest honor: **OJP Champion**.

### The Progression Levels

Each track follows a three-level progression that reflects growing impact and commitment:

**Entry Level**: Your first meaningful contribution. You've made a concrete impactâ€”merged a PR, reported and validated bugs, improved documentation, or published content about OJP. Entry badges recognize that you've crossed the threshold from user to contributor.

**Intermediate Level**: Sustained contributions over time. You're not just a one-time contributor; you've returned multiple times, engaged in discussions, helped others, and demonstrated consistent quality. Intermediate badges show you're an active member of the community.

**Advanced Level**: Leadership and expertise. You've become a trusted voice in your trackâ€”maintaining significant code areas, shaping testing strategy, building comprehensive learning resources, or organizing community initiatives. Advanced badges represent mastery and sustained impact.

The **OJP Champion** badge represents the highest recognition across all tracks. Champions are contributors who've made exceptional, sustained impactâ€”often across multiple tracks. They mentor others, guide project direction, and are broadly recognized as community leaders.

You reach Champion status through:
- High-impact contributions in one or more tracks
- Sustained engagement over time (not just a burst of activity)
- Active mentorship or inspiration of other contributors
- Broad recognition by the community as a leader

Champions may contribute primarily in one track (like a prolific code contributor) or across multiple tracks (like someone who codes, documents, and evangelizes). What matters is the exceptional level of impact and leadership.

## The Nomination and Award Process

The OJP Recognition Program balances structure with flexibility, ensuring badges reflect real impact while remaining accessible and fair.

### Who Can Nominate

Any current OJP badge holder can nominate another contributor for any badge. This peer-based system ensures nominations come from people who understand the project and have seen the nominee's contributions firsthand.

You don't need to hold an advanced badge to nominate someone for an advanced badge. If you're an OJP Contributor (entry level) and you've worked with someone who clearly deserves recognition as an OJP Developer (intermediate level), your nomination carries weight.

### How Nominations Work

Nominations are informal but should include:
- **Nominee's name and GitHub username** (or LinkedIn profile)
- **Badge being nominated for** (specify track and level)
- **Rationale**: Briefly describe the contributions that merit recognitionâ€”specific PRs, documentation, talks, community engagement, etc.
- **Your relationship to the work**: How you know about these contributions (collaborated on PRs, saw talks, used documentation, etc.)

Submit nominations by:
- Creating an issue in the OJP repository with the label "badge-nomination"
- Emailing the project founder directly
- Mentioning nominations in OJP community discussions or meetings

### Review and Discretion

Nominations are reviewed by the **project founder** and current **OJP Champions**. This group has discretion to:

**Accept nominations**: If the contributions clearly meet the criteria and the community feedback is positive, badges are awarded promptly.

**Request more information**: Sometimes reviewers need additional context about specific contributions or impact.

**Decline nominations**: If contributions don't yet meet the criteria for the nominated badge level, reviewers may suggest nominating for a different level or encouraging more contributions before re-nominating.

**Directly award badges**: The founder and Champions can identify contributors whose work clearly merits recognition and award badges without waiting for a formal nomination. This often happens when reviewing PRs or community contributions.

### Recognition Without Bureaucracy

The process intentionally avoids rigid checklists. While the badge criteria provide guidelines, reviewers consider:

- **Sustained vs. burst activity**: Has the contributor engaged over time, or just during a single sprint?
- **Impact beyond quantity**: A single high-impact PR might outweigh many trivial changes.
- **Community spirit**: Does the contributor collaborate well, help others, and embody open-source values?
- **Cross-track contributions**: Contributions spanning multiple tracks (like coding + documentation) may accelerate progression toward Champion status.

This discretion ensures badges represent genuine merit, not just meeting arbitrary thresholds.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I nominate myself for a badge?**
A: Self-nominations are discouraged, as recognition is most meaningful when it comes from peers who've observed your work. However, if you believe you've made significant contributions that haven't been noticed, you can reach out to the project founder to discuss your involvement.

**Q: How long does the review process take?**
A: Most nominations are reviewed within 1-2 weeks. Complex cases or borderline decisions may take longer as reviewers gather community feedback.

**Q: What if I disagree with a declined nomination?**
A: Reviewers provide feedback on why nominations were declined and what would make a future nomination successful. Use this as guidance for where to focus your contributions. You can be re-nominated later after making the suggested improvements.

**Q: Can badges be revoked?**
A: In extreme casesâ€”like plagiarism, code of conduct violations, or misrepresenting contributionsâ€”badges can be revoked. This is rare and requires clear evidence of misconduct. Simply becoming less active after earning a badge does not result in revocation.

**Q: Do badges expire?**
A: No. Once earned, badges are yours permanently. They represent contributions you made at a point in time and remain valid even if you later become less active in the project.

**Q: Can I earn multiple badges in the same track?**
A: Yes! If you progress from Contributor â†’ Developer â†’ Core Developer in the code track, you've earned all three badges. Each level supersedes the previous one in terms of recognition, but you can reference any or all of them when discussing your contributions.

**Q: How does the Champion badge work if I contribute across multiple tracks?**
A: Champion status considers your total impact across all tracks. You don't need to reach Advanced level in all tracksâ€”you might be Advanced in one track and Intermediate in another, with that combination of sustained high-impact work qualifying you for Champion status.

**Q: Is there a minimum time commitment to earn a badge?**
A: No specific time requirement exists. Some contributors earn entry-level badges after a few weeks of focused contribution. Advanced badges typically require months of sustained engagement, but there's no rigid timeline. What matters is impact, not calendar time.
